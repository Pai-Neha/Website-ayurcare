Dos and Don'ts for Asthma

Do:

  • Follow your asthma action plan provided by your healthcare provider.
  • Take prescribed asthma medications as directed, including inhalers and controller medications.
  • Avoid asthma triggers like smoke, dust mites, pollen, and pet dander.
  • Use a peak flow meter to monitor your lung function regularly.
  • Stay hydrated to keep airways moist and mucus thin.
  • Learn and practice proper breathing techniques, such as diaphragmatic breathing.
  • Exercise regularly to improve lung function, but consult your doctor for suitable activities.
  • Create an asthma-friendly home environment with proper ventilation and cleanliness.
  • Teach family members and close contacts about asthma and what to do in an emergency.

Don't:

  • Avoid smoking or exposure to secondhand smoke at all costs.
  • Avoid vigorous physical activities in cold or dry weather without proper precautions.
  • Don't skip your regular doctor appointments or medication doses, even when feeling well.
  • Limit exposure to air pollution and industrial irritants if possible.
  • Don't rely solely on rescue inhalers; use controller medications as prescribed.
  • Avoid excessive consumption of sulfite-containing foods, which can trigger asthma symptoms (e.g., dried fruits, wine).
  • Limit heavy perfumes or fragrances in your surroundings.
  • Avoid contact with pets if you are allergic to them; consider hypoallergenic pets if desired.
  • Don't ignore worsening symptoms or signs of an asthma attack; take action promptly.

Symptoms and Management

Symptoms:

  • Shortness of breath and wheezing.
  • Coughing, especially at night or in the early morning.
  • Tightness in the chest and difficulty breathing properly.
  • Increased mucus production in the airways.
  • Reduced lung function, as indicated by peak flow measurements.
  • Frequent asthma exacerbations or attacks.
  • Restlessness and anxiety due to breathing difficulties.
  • Pale or bluish complexion (a sign of severe oxygen deficiency).
  • Difficulty speaking in full sentences due to shortness of breath.
  • Use of accessory muscles in the neck and chest to breathe (an emergency sign).

Management:

  • Work with your healthcare provider to create a personalized asthma action plan.
  • Take your prescribed medications as directed and know how to use inhalers correctly.
  • Identify and avoid asthma triggers, such as allergens and irritants in your environment.
  • Monitor your lung function with a peak flow meter or spirometry regularly.
  • Seek prompt medical attention during severe asthma attacks or worsening symptoms.
  • Follow a balanced and healthy diet to support overall lung and immune health.
  • Stay physically active and fit, tailored to your asthma condition, and consult your healthcare provider.
  • Educate yourself about asthma management and share this knowledge with family and caregivers.
  • Practice stress-reduction techniques like deep breathing and relaxation to manage anxiety and asthma-related stress.
  • Consider allergy testing and immunotherapy if allergies are a significant trigger for your asthma.

Additional Information

Asthma is a chronic respiratory condition that affects the airways in the lungs, causing them to narrow and become inflamed. While it can be managed effectively, it's essential for individuals with asthma to follow their healthcare provider's guidance closely. Proper management, including the use of controller and rescue medications, avoidance of triggers, and lifestyle adjustments, can help individuals lead a healthy life with asthma. Awareness and education about asthma are crucial for both individuals with asthma and their caregivers.
